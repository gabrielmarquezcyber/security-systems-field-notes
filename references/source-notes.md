## Cybersecurity workforce shortage / shortage paradox

- BLS, *Information Security Analysts: Occupational Outlook Handbook*
  https://www.bls.gov/ooh/computer-and-information-technology/information-security-analysts.htm

Key fact: Employment of information security analysts is projected to grow 29 percent from 2024 to 2034, with about 16,000 openings projected each year on average.

Use carefully: Occupational demand is strong, but projected occupational growth does not equal clean entry-level conversion.

- ISACA, *State of Cybersecurity 2025* press release
  https://www.isaca.org/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/2025/state-of-cybersecurity-2025-global-press-release

Key facts: 55 percent of cybersecurity teams are understaffed; 65 percent have unfilled cybersecurity positions; 38 percent say it takes three to six months to hire for entry-level roles; 70 percent expect demand for technical cybersecurity contributors to rise.

Use carefully: Supports the shortage paradox and the distinction between demand, hiring friction, and training/formation failure.

- ISC2, *2025 Cybersecurity Workforce Study*
  https://www.isc2.org/Insights/2025/12/2025-ISC2-Cybersecurity-Workforce-Study

Key point: ISC2 did not publish a workforce gap estimate in 2025, emphasizing instead that specific skills needs have become more important than a raw people shortage. ISC2 also notes that shortages and budget constraints affect security resilience.

Use carefully: Useful for arguing that "shortage" may mean skills, training, budget, and formation problems rather than merely a lack of applicants.

- CyberSeek, June 2025 update
  https://www.cyberseek.org/docs/06-02-2025_CyberSeek_June_2025.pdf

Key facts: CyberSeek-calculated supply-demand ratio was 74 percent, and recruiting periods were 21 percent longer on average.

Use carefully: Supports persistent labor-market friction even with high demand.

- Fortinet, *2026 Global Cybersecurity Skills Gap Report* press release
  https://www.fortinet.com/corporate/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/2026/fortinet-report-reveals-cybersecurity-hiring-stalls-as-nearly-half-of-it-leaders-face-corporate-pushback

Key facts: 56 percent of IT leaders cite lack of cybersecurity skills as a top cause of breaches; 51 percent say senior-level cybersecurity skills are most needed; 49 percent struggle to get approval for additional cybersecurity talent.

Use carefully: Supports the argument that security demand can coexist with budget constraints and seniority-biased hiring.

- Fortinet, *2025 Cybersecurity Skills Gap Global Research Report*
  https://www.fortinet.com/content/dam/fortinet/assets/reports/2025-cybersecurity-skills-gap-report.pdf

Key facts: Lack of IT security skills and training is cited as a top breach cause; lack of staff with sufficient AI expertise is a major challenge to AI integration in cybersecurity.

Use carefully: Supports the AI/security skills layer.

## Labor-market degradation / older work ladders

- BLS, *Forty years of falling manufacturing employment*
  https://www.bls.gov/opub/btn/volume-9/forty-years-of-falling-manufacturing-employment.htm

Key fact: Manufacturing employment reached an all-time peak of 19.6 million in June 1979. In June 2019, it was 12.8 million, down 6.7 million or 35 percent from the peak.

- BLS, *15 percent of private industry workers had access to a defined benefit retirement plan*
  https://www.bls.gov/opub/ted/2024/15-percent-of-private-industry-workers-had-access-to-a-defined-benefit-retirement-plan.htm

Key fact: In March 2023, 15 percent of private industry workers had access to a defined benefit retirement plan.

- BLS, *Customer Service Representatives: Occupational Outlook Handbook*
  https://www.bls.gov/ooh/office-and-administrative-support/customer-service-representatives.htm

Key facts: Employment of customer service representatives is projected to decline 5 percent from 2024 to 2034; median hourly wage was $20.59 in May 2024.

Use carefully: Supports the argument that domestic support work is not expanding as a stable ladder.

## Male labor-force participation / broader social symptom

- Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco, *Men's Falling Labor Force Participation across Generations*
  https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2023/10/mens-falling-labor-force-participation-across-generations/

Key point: Prime-age male labor-force participation has declined for decades; about 14 percent of millennial males at age 25 were not in the labor force compared with 7 percent of baby boomer males at that age.

- FRED / BLS, *Labor Force Participation Rate*
  https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/CIVPART

Key fact: Use for current labor-force participation context.

- Fortune, June 2026, male labor-force participation and childhood exposure to weak labor markets
  https://fortune.com/2026/06/21/male-labor-force-participation-rate-decline-childhood-experience-wages-unemployment/

Use as current hook, not as sole source.

## Global labor / offshoring / skilled migration

- FCC, *Report and Order, Further Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, and Notice of Proposed Rulemaking*, FCC-26-16A1
  https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/FCC-26-16A1.pdf

Use carefully: Supports the claim that offshore call centers, agent location disclosure, English proficiency, and call-handling location are live policy topics.

- U.S. Department of Labor, H-1B Program
  https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/immigration/h1b

Key definition: H-1B applies to employers seeking to hire workers in specialty occupations requiring highly specialized knowledge and at least a bachelor's degree or equivalent.

- Pew Research Center, *What we know about the U.S. H-1B visa program*
  https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/03/04/what-we-know-about-the-us-h-1b-visa-program/

Key point: Computer-related jobs have been the most common occupation for H-1B workers for more than a decade; since fiscal 2012, about 60 percent or more of H-1B workers approved each year held computer-related jobs.

## Public language guardrail

Do not frame global labor analysis as blame toward immigrants or foreign workers.

Use:

```text
global labor arbitrage
offshoring
nearshoring
remote labor markets
vendor substitution
cost-optimized support models
domestic middle-class wage pressure
high-skill immigration pathways
global applicant pools
selection-interface problem
```

Avoid:

```text
foreigners are taking jobs
invasion language
cheap foreign labor as insult
anti-immigrant phrasing
```

Core public-safe formulation:

```text
The American worker is increasingly competing inside a global cost-optimization system while still carrying American housing, healthcare, debt, family, and cost-of-living burdens.
```
