# Making Competence Inspectable

## Cybersecurity Hiring in a Globalized Signal Economy

> Spanish version: [Making Competence Inspectable — Español](making-competence-inspectable-es.md)

The modern labor market is usually discussed as a test of personal attitude. If someone cannot find work in the field they trained for, the questions arrive quickly enough: Did they network enough? Choose the wrong degree? Fail to specialize? Need one more certification, one more project, one more revision of the resume?

Some of those questions are fair. None of them are sufficient.

A more accurate image is musical chairs played at national scale, but not the tidy children's version where one chair disappears and everyone politely accepts the result. In the labor-market version, the music stops when a real shock hits the room. Sometimes it stops for everyone at once: a recession, a financial crisis, a pandemic, a technological shift, an offshoring wave. Too many people lunge for too few seats. Some are left standing. Some are knocked to the floor in the scramble. Some end up locked in dispute over the same chair while an authority decides who has the stronger claim, and others are told to keep circling after the round has already been decided.

At other times, the silence is more private and almost invisible. A budget quietly freezes. A resume disappears inside an applicant tracking system before a human ever reads it. A role advertised as "entry-level" demands the precise experience it once existed to provide. The room may not even notice that the music stopped for one person, but for that person the result is the same: the instructions continue, the motion continues, and the seat is already gone.

The official story begins with the person left standing and asks what they did wrong. This essay begins with the room: the missing chairs, the damaged ladders, the rule changes between rounds, and the institutions that continue to explain structural chaos as an individual failure of attitude.

Cybersecurity hiring is the immediate case study, but it is not merely another example. It is one of the clearest microcosms of a deeper crisis: the problem of making human ability visible after the older institutions that once formed, tested, and recognized that ability have thinned out. The claim here is not that a portfolio or work sample is magic. A GitHub repository does not guarantee a seat. In a broken selection environment, proof itself can devolve into another unpaid ritual, one more hoop candidates are asked to jump through while waiting for someone with authority to notice.

Still, the demand for visible proof deserves serious analysis because it responds to a real limitation: modern hiring frequently cannot observe competence directly. We have built a hiring apparatus largely out of proxies. A credential records that someone studied; a resume records what someone is willing to claim. A referral transfers borrowed trust, while an interview tests how well a person performs under artificial observation. A serious work sample speaks a different language. It says: here is how this person reasons when the evidence is incomplete, the constraints are real, and the decision still has to be made.

That is not everything a hiring system needs. But in a labor market where trust is expensive, generic output is cheap, and older ladders no longer convert cleanly into new opportunities, making competence inspectable may be one of the few remaining ways to give judgment a surface area before the next round begins.



## The shortage that does not feel like a door

We are told, relentlessly, that cybersecurity faces a catastrophic talent shortage. The raw data hums that tune: the Bureau of Labor Statistics projects employment for information security analysts to grow 29 percent from 2024 to 2034, with about 16,000 openings projected each year, while industry sources such as CyberSeek and Fortinet continue to warn about understaffed teams, unmet demand, and organizations struggling to secure the headcount they say they need.[^cyber-demand]

Yet from inside the job hunt, this shortage rarely feels like an open door. It feels like a glass wall.

The contradiction begins to dissolve once the word shortage is forced to do less work. What the industry often suffers from is a constellation of specific deficits: a shortage of seasoned veterans who can take production risk on day one, a shortage of approved budgets, a shortage of managers with the bandwidth and authority to train, a shortage of teams willing to translate adjacent experience into security capability, and a profound shortage of institutional courage. Some organizations need judgment but screen for title-matching experience because title is easier to process than judgment. Some genuinely need help but have built no structure for absorbing anyone who is not already institutionally safe.

This is where much of the motivational language around cybersecurity becomes misleading. It is true that the field is growing, but an occupation can grow at the macro level while remaining difficult to enter at the point where selection actually happens. Growth projections do not explain why candidates with degrees, certifications, labs, support backgrounds, and real technical habits still struggle to convert. They do not explain why teams can describe themselves as understaffed while rejecting the people they would need to develop, or why companies can complain about cyber risk while waiting for perfect candidates instead of forming plausible ones.

When a company laments its exposure while refusing to hire anyone but the flawless, zero-risk applicant, it is not merely experiencing a talent shortage. It is experiencing a crisis of formation, a quiet refusal to build the very successors the profession requires to survive. A shortage can be produced by the absence of people, but it can also be manufactured by the refusal to absorb learners.



## The ladders under the ladder

The cybersecurity hiring problem does not exist in isolation. It sits inside a larger reorganization of work that has been underway for decades. It is tempting to treat every contemporary career difficulty as a personal-branding failure because branding is the variable an individual can still attempt to control. The harder truth is that the room itself changed before many candidates walked into it, and some of the chairs were already gone before the new players understood the rules.

The older economy was not a romance. Much of the work was dangerous, physically punishing, poorly paid, repetitive, and socially invisible. Yet, for all its exhaustion, it offered ordinary people recognizable architecture: manufacturing floors, retail commissions with internal promotions, technical support desks, local trades, warehouse routes, union halls, and pensioned employment. These ladders did not guarantee dignity, but they offered something structurally essential: a mechanism, however imperfect, for converting labor into adulthood, family formation, and a local sense of usefulness. That path has narrowed.

The scale of that narrowing is not imaginary. Manufacturing employment reached a peak of 19.6 million in June 1979. By June 2019 it had fallen to 12.8 million, a decline of 6.7 million jobs.[^manufacturing-retail-csr] Retail trade is projected by BLS to lose more jobs than any other sector from 2024 to 2034, shaped by automation, consolidation, and e-commerce. Customer service representative employment is projected to decline as well.[^manufacturing-retail-csr] These are not just occupational statistics. They are changes in the number of places where ordinary people can become useful, visible, and trusted over time.

Economists and social theorists have different languages for this. Schumpeter called capitalism's internal churn creative destruction, a phrase that can sound almost elegant from a distance.[^creative-destruction] Innovation creates new industries, breaks old arrangements, and rearranges the economy from within. But destruction is not evenly distributed. Autor, Dorn, and Hanson showed how trade shocks, labor-market polarization, and geography can concentrate the costs of economic change in particular places and among particular workers.[^autor-dorn-hanson] From the worker's side, creative destruction does not arrive as a clean abstraction. It arrives as the closed store, the vanished management track, the outsourced support desk, and the local ladder that no longer teaches the next skill.

These structural fractures did not arrive as a single, clean historical event; they compounded over decades. The post-9/11 security shock, the 2008 financial collapse, and the sudden physical closures of the COVID-19 pandemic did not merely pause the economy. They reorganized it. Travel, tourism, hospitality, retail, local businesses, management tracks, and family balance sheets all absorbed shocks that later hardened into operating assumptions.

At the national level, these were moments when the music really did stop. The room panicked because too many claims were suddenly competing for too few stable seats. After 2008, the language of emergency became public and obscene: quantitative easing, stimulus, bailouts, toxic assets, too big to fail. Some interventions may have prevented wider collapse, but they also taught a generation a brutal lesson about the rules of the room. Institutions with systemic importance could be stabilized in the name of necessity, while households, workers, and young adults were told to adapt, retrain, stay positive, and keep moving. The moral atmosphere that later made Occupy Wall Street intelligible did not come from nowhere. It came from watching the referee protect certain chairs while ordinary people fought for whatever remained.[^post-2008]

For the working and middle classes, these events were not experienced as isolated crises to be weathered and then forgotten. They worked more like a mechanical ratchet: the immediate panic faded, the emergency cuts remained, the temporary lean-staffing model hardened into a permanent baseline, and by the time the economy "recovered," the ladder had already been narrowed. The diminished normal became the starting point for the next crisis.

That is why the decline of a work ladder is not just a wage story. It is a story of lost translation, of workers realizing that the market no longer knows how to read the competence it once rewarded. A person can be promoted, trusted, and useful inside one institution, then discover that the next market cannot translate that history into present value. The channel closes; the company restructures; the work moves to a vendor; the business fails. The resume still lists real experience, but the language of the room has changed.

Sometimes the cruelty is sharper. A firm may use a worker's competence to standardize the process, document the workflow, stabilize the channel, and make the work cheaper, more scripted, more vendor-ready, more offshore-ready, or more automatable. In that case, competence did not fail. Competence succeeded so well that the institution learned how to remove the chair and tell the person who built it to keep circling.

This is why "people do not want to work" is an unserious explanation for the present. Millions of people have already adapted through multiple destroyed ladders. The bridge between work and reward changed beneath them.


## The degree and the participation ticket

For many working-class and immigrant families, education was the honorable exit path. The bargain was simple enough to build a life around: work hard, study, get the degree, move into the professional world, escape the body-breaking insecurity of the previous generation, and help the family rise.

That dream was not foolish. It worked for many people, and it still works for some. But the chair that education was supposed to reserve is no longer as reliable as the story suggests.

The problem is not that college has no value. On average, graduates still often retain wage and long-term advantages over non-graduates. The problem is the first conversion point: the move from degree to professional employment. The Burning Glass Institute's 2025 report on young graduates argues that, for the first time in modern history, a bachelor's degree is no longer a reliable path to professional employment. It points to a combination of AI pressure on junior tasks, lean post-pandemic staffing, risk-averse hiring, and a growing supply of graduates. Strada and Burning Glass research found that 52 percent of graduates were underemployed a year after graduation and 45 percent remained underemployed a decade later. The New York Fed's recent graduate tracker showed an underemployment rate above 40 percent in early 2026.[^graduate-underemployment]

The joke about the philosopher working at Starbucks lands because it contains a real mismatch: education without a fitting chair. It is funny until it becomes a social pattern. Then it becomes cruel. The philosopher at Starbucks is not just someone who planned badly. He is evidence that the room changed.

The degree problem is also a cost-of-life problem. The useful concept is not whether a person is poor by one official threshold. The useful concept is the participation ticket. Michael Green's recent argument about a higher "real poverty line" is controversial, and the exact number should not be treated as settled.[^participation-ticket] But the controversy points at something the official poverty line often hides: the cost of ordinary participation has expanded beyond food, shelter, and bare survival. What does it actually cost to participate in middle-class life today? We are not talking about luxury. We are talking about the baseline requirements for civic and economic existence: a safe place to live, reliable transportation, health care, child care, broadband, and enough geographic mobility to reach work. It is the stability to form a household, the financial slack to absorb a setback without collapsing, and the ability to raise children without every unexpected expense mutating into a crisis.

That is the chair many families thought education would reserve for their children. It became harder to keep not only because wages changed, but because the cost of sitting down changed.

This does not mean everyone should abandon intellectual life and run toward the same set of practical fields. A serious society needs builders, nurses, electricians, analysts, mechanics, teachers, historians, artists, writers, translators, and public thinkers. If everyone rushes into the same supposedly safe room, the next room becomes overcrowded, and the same fight over too few seats begins again. The problem is not that practical skill is bad. The problem is that individual skill selection cannot, by itself, solve a structural shortage of stable entry points.

Education was sold as a ladder. For too many people, it has become a ticket to stand in a longer line.

## A personal microcosm, not a private complaint

There is no reason to pretend the example that follows is not personal. My own work history is visible on LinkedIn; the family-business context is known to people close to me. I am not presenting it as exceptional. I am using it because a single trajectory can sometimes make visible what aggregate numbers only describe.

By the time I walked onto the floor at Best Buy in the early 2010s, the surrounding consumer-electronics retail ecosystem was already a graveyard. Circuit City had liquidated; RadioShack was moving toward its own descent into bankruptcy. Smaller local shops had been squeezed by e-commerce, price transparency, big-box scale, and changing consumer habits. A massive surviving employer can look, from the outside, like a beacon of opportunity after the competitors have vanished. From inside the industry, consolidation means something colder: fewer gates, narrower paths, and less room for workers to become visible outside the surviving giant.

I did not enter through a purely meritocratic filter. A human connection inside one store pulled my application into view. That fact is neither embarrassing nor especially unusual. It is simply how many actual hiring decisions still occur: someone with context decides that a person is worth seeing. Once visible, competence mattered. I performed, advanced into remote technical support, and eventually reached compensation that compared favorably with many retail supervisory roles at the time.

The connection did not perform the work. It created the condition under which work could be evaluated. After that, performance was decisive.

But the channel itself was already narrowing. Remote technical support, which had once represented a domestic technical ladder inside a retail company, was increasingly moved toward outsourcing and vendor models. In 2017 Best Buy eliminated roughly four hundred remote Geek Squad positions with the explicit goal of shifting that work outward.[^best-buy-geek-squad] For the people inside that function, the change did not register as an abstract efficiency gain. It registered as the institution extracting domestic competence and then relocating the work into a lower-cost operating model.

That sequence - human trust creating visibility, competence producing advancement, consolidation narrowing the field, outsourcing removing the ladder - is not unique to one company or one worker. It is a small map of a larger reorganization.

The worker experiences that sequence as wage pressure and displacement. The consumer experiences it as scripted support, escalation loops, vendor fragmentation, language friction, and the feeling that no one with ownership of the problem is accountable. The corporation experiences it as cost optimization. All three experiences belong to the same structure.

## The global signal economy

The modern labor market has not merely grown more competitive. It has become hyper-mediated, increasingly borderless, and ruthlessly optimized for cost. A domestic applicant now enters an arena where local peers are only one part of the field; depending on the role, he may also be competing with offshore vendors, nearshore service centers, foreign worker programs, contract firms, global remote applicants, internal transfers, AI-assisted workers, and other domestic candidates who have accumulated the same degrees, certifications, and polished profiles.

This is the part of the conversation most likely to be distorted, so it has to be stated carefully. The point is not to blame the foreign worker who takes the job. It is to understand the incentive structure in which the domestic worker is trying to become visible.

The American worker is caught in a punishing paradox: he must compete inside a frictionless global cost-optimization machine while still carrying the heavy, localized costs of American housing, health care, debt, transportation, and family formation. The labor price is pulled toward a global auction; the rent, the insurance bill, the car note, the student loan, and the family obligations remain stubbornly local. That gap between global labor pricing and domestic life pricing is not a side issue. It is one of the hidden pressures underneath the modern professional scramble.

This dynamic lands with particular force on immigrants and their children. The classic immigrant bargain is an intergenerational pact: cross the border, absorb the risk, take the grueling, foundational work, and build the platform so the next generation can cross the threshold into the professional class. But today that second generation is arriving at the professional gates just as those gates are being automated, offshored, saturated with credentials, and barricaded by risk-averse algorithms. The family completes the generational climb, only to discover that the ladder has been moved.

Computer-related occupations have been the most common category for H-1B workers for more than a decade, and federal debates around offshore call centers show that service location, language, transfer rights, and consumer accountability are active public-policy questions.[^global-labor] Again, the point is not resentment toward workers. It is clarity about the selection environment. The domestic candidate is not entering a purely local labor market. He is entering a global economy of signals.

In that environment, the advice to "just get more qualified" becomes incomplete. The candidate is not simply trying to become competent. He is trying to become legible.

Large systems simplify reality in order to administer it, and modern hiring does exactly this.[^legibility] It cannot see the whole human being, so it maps the person into flattened, digestible signals: title, years of experience, degree, keyword match, tool list, prior employer, location, authorization, referral, salary range, and public profile. Those signals help institutions process risk, but they also flatten people. Security work often depends on traits that are harder to read from the map: judgment, restraint, escalation discipline, uncertainty handling, evidence interpretation, and the ability to communicate risk without exaggeration.

A candidate can become more competent without becoming more legible. That is one of the central traps.

The next pressure is attention. In an information-rich environment, human attention becomes the scarcest resource, and modern hiring is an attention-poor system pretending to be an information-rich one.[^attention-scarcity] A candidate may add evidence, but the system may not have enough attention to interpret it. Proof has to be real, but it also has to be reviewable. It has to carry meaning across a narrow channel.

In an image-mediated professional world, the danger is that proof devolves into spectacle.[^spectacle] LinkedIn can turn the job search into a theater of employability: images of discipline, images of resilience, images of hunger, images of being the sort of candidate the market claims to want. None of that is automatically false. The danger is that image begins to substitute for inspection. A serious artifact has to resist that corporate theater. It cannot simply say, "Look at me learning." It has to say, "Here is how I reason through evidence when the decision actually matters."

## AI makes generic proof cheap

The arrival of generative AI makes the signal problem acutely urgent. The exact long-term employment effects remain fiercely debated, but the immediate consequence for the job seeker is already here: AI drastically cheapens generic proof. The IMF has estimated that about 60 percent of jobs in advanced economies are exposed to AI. Goldman Sachs has estimated that the equivalent of 300 million jobs globally are exposed to automation. The World Economic Forum projects major job churn by 2030, and McKinsey expects additional occupational transitions as generative AI accelerates automation.[^ai-exposure]

The optimistic story says AI gives workers leverage. The darker story says AI gives firms a new ratchet: fewer entry-level roles, more senior-ready expectations, more contractorized work, more surveillance, more automated support, and more pressure on candidates to arrive already trained. Both stories can be true in different places.

For hiring, the narrower point is already enough. When algorithms can instantly generate polished resumes, flawless cover letters, clean diagrams, plausible scripts, lab summaries, and code snippets at scale, ordinary signals lose contrast. That does not make all AI-assisted work fake. It means the question changes. A manager is no longer asking only, "Did this candidate produce something?" The better question is, "Does this artifact expose human judgment?"

A serious artifact must show exactly what generic output is designed to hide: the messy constraints, the missing evidence, the calculated tradeoffs, the uncertainty, and the limits of the author's knowledge. It should show where the tool helped and where the person took responsibility. It should show what the person noticed, what the person ignored, what the person escalated, and what the person would change after review. In security work, that matters more than a screenshot of a completed lab or a repository full of unexplained files.

The future of hiring may not rest on whether a candidate knows how to use AI. It may rest on whether the candidate knows where AI must stop and where human consequence begins.



## Ritual or artifact?

There is a legitimate worry that portfolio-building can become another anxious professional ritual: another unpaid performance demanded of people who already feel precarious. Seen through Joseph Tainter's lens, this is what complexity looks like when it stops solving problems and starts protecting the process itself.[^tainter] Hiring has its own version of that pattern. Applicant tracking systems, recruiter screens, keyword optimization, take-home exercises, personality assessments, culture-fit language, interview loops, portfolio requirements, LinkedIn activity metrics, and now AI-generated application materials can accumulate until the process is elaborate without being wise.

This is part of why so much contemporary career language feels thin. Harry Frankfurt's account of bullshit helps explain the deadness of the register: the problem is not always that the words are false, but that they become indifferent to truth.[^frankfurt] Candidates are instructed to appear passionate but not desperate, authentic but not inconvenient, confident but not demanding, humble but not uncertain, serious but unfailingly upbeat. Something stranger than lying happens: the language detaches from the real problem it claims to describe.

David Graeber's work points toward the institutional side of the same unease.[^graeber] Organizations can preserve roles, rituals, and vocabularies that maintain form while producing unclear value. A hiring system can announce talent, culture, opportunity, and growth while making the actual human being visible only in the forms the process has already decided it can safely accommodate.

A useful artifact pushes against that flattening. It stays in contact with the difficulty of the work rather than the performance of employability.

If no one inspects it, proof becomes a digital archive. If no one understands it, proof becomes noise. If it never interfaces with a decision, proof becomes self-expression. Those are not only failures of the candidate; they are failures of distribution, timing, translation, network, and selection environment. In the language of the larger metaphor, the work has to reach someone with authority over a seat. The difference between a hollow ritual and an operational asset lies in whether the work enters a human decision loop.


## What inspectable competence actually means

Cybersecurity hiring does not select for competence directly; it selects through defensive layers, and those layers are not neutral truth machines. Modern institutions filter decisions through authority, budget, reputation, risk, and organizational self-protection. Hiring is no longer simply a search for talent. It is a managed risk decision inside a bureaucracy that must be able to justify itself.

That is why each layer rewards a different signal. An applicant tracking system rewards keyword density. A recruiter rewards communication clarity, salary alignment, and low-risk presentation. A hiring manager rewards reduced operational burden. A technical reviewer rewards documented reasoning. A network rewards trust transfer. A platform rewards visibility. A corporate culture rewards emotional fit.

Each layer demands a different frequency of signal, which explains the central trap of the modern job hunt: a candidate can become massively more competent without becoming any more selected. The brutal, unspoken question of the market is not, "Are you smart enough?" It is, "Can I see enough evidence, trust, sponsorship, polish, and context to justify taking a risk on you?"

Making competence inspectable is not the same thing as building artifacts and waiting. A better formulation is to convert competence into trust-bearing signals. Personnel-selection research has long treated work samples as one way to observe what a person can do, rather than only what the person claims, studied, or previously titled.[^work-samples] A good work sample is not generic content. It is job-relevant evidence under constraints.

In cybersecurity, that might be an incident-analysis narrative, a detection decision, an identity-risk review, a cloud-access investigation, a vulnerability prioritization memo, or an AI-tool-boundary analysis. The format matters less than the exposed judgment. The artifact should make the reader believe, "I can see how this person thinks."

A serious portfolio is also a strange hybrid of exit, voice, and loyalty: exit from total dependence on credentials, voice directed at a hiring system that cannot see enough, and loyalty to the idea that competence still deserves a chance to matter.[^hirschman] It also introduces a kind of skin in the game. A serious artifact exposes judgment.[^skin-in-the-game] It can be inspected, challenged, improved, rejected, or used. It puts some part of the author's reasoning at risk.

A credential proves that you studied. A resume proves what you are willing to claim. A referral is a transfer of borrowed trust. An interview tests how well you perform under artificial evaluation. But a serious artifact cuts through the proxy layer to say something else entirely: here is how I reason when the evidence is incomplete, the stakes are real, and the decision still matters.

Making competence inspectable is not a magic bullet. It will not summon a chair where one does not exist, repair the fractured global labor market, compel institutions to train, neutralize the effects of generative AI on entry-level formation, or make hiring systems consistently fair. It is a narrower intervention. In an environment where older mechanisms for recognizing ability have weakened, where generic output is inexpensive to produce, and where many selection processes cannot directly observe judgment, a serious work sample gives competence a surface. It gives a reviewer something concrete to inspect. It gives trust a place to attach.

The music will stop again. When it does, some people will be left standing, some will be knocked down in the scramble, and some will be trapped in contests over seats that no longer have clear rules of possession. Proof does not repair the room. But if the referee cannot see who can actually play, proof is one of the few ways left to make judgment visible before the next round is decided.
