# LinkedIn Excerpt - Making Competence Inspectable

Cybersecurity is supposed to have a talent shortage.

At the same time, many candidates experience the entry interface as brutal.

That contradiction is the point.

A field can grow at the occupational level while remaining difficult to enter at the selection-interface level. There can be chairs somewhere in the room while the person circling still cannot reach one.

The usual advice is not necessarily wrong:

Get the certification.
Build the lab.
Create the portfolio.
Network.
Keep going.

But the advice often skips the harder question: what problem is a portfolio supposed to solve?

A credential says I studied.

A resume says I claim.

A referral says someone trusts me.

An interview says I can perform under evaluation.

A serious work sample says: here is how I think when evidence is incomplete and the decision still matters.

That is not magic. It does not guarantee a job. It may even become another unpaid ritual candidates perform while waiting to be seen.

But in a globalized signal economy where older work ladders have degraded, hiring systems are mediated, AI has cheapened generic content, and trust is scarce, making competence inspectable may be one of the few rational experiments left.

Not because it guarantees a chair.

Because it gives trust something real to inspect.
